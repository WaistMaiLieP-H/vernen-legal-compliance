## Audit Method

The original analysis identified 6 audit findings from Stockton's FY2024 Single Audit and mapped them against HUD CPD monitoring risk indicators. This re-audit applies the full triple constraint to identify what the original analysis missed:

1. **Governing Guidelines** — Does the City comply with the law? (original 6 findings)
2. **Standards of Creation** — Were the City's compliance documents created according to the professional standards that define how they must be made?
3. **SOC Controls** — Can the City verify the integrity of its compliance documentation?

---

## PART 1: GOVERNING GUIDELINES — Original 6 Findings

### Finding 1 (2024-004): CDBG — Allowable Costs / Payroll Expenditure Controls
**Severity: HIGH**
**Regulation:** 2 CFR 200.403 (Allowable Costs); 2 CFR 200.430 (Compensation)

The City did not maintain adequate internal controls over payroll expenditures charged to CDBG. Costs charged to federal programs must be necessary, reasonable, and allocable. Without payroll controls, the City cannot demonstrate that staff time charged to CDBG was actually spent on CDBG activities.

---

### Finding 2 (2024-005): CDBG — Procurement / SAM Verification Failures
**Severity: CRITICAL**
**Regulation:** 2 CFR 200.214 (Suspension and Debarment); 31 USC 3729 (False Claims Act)

The City did not verify vendors against SAM.gov prior to awarding CDBG-funded procurements. If any federal funds went to a suspended or debarred entity, this is not a control deficiency — it is potential False Claims Act exposure.

---

### Finding 3 (2024-006): CDBG — Reporting / Internal Control Over Federal Reports
**Severity: HIGH**
**Regulation:** 2 CFR 200.328 (Financial Reporting); 2 CFR 200.303 (Internal Controls)

The City's internal controls were not designed to detect errors in federal financial reports submitted to HUD. Reports may contain inaccuracies that the City cannot identify before submission.

---

### Finding 4 (2024-007): CDBG — Environmental Reviews / Special Tests
**Severity: HIGH**
**Regulation:** 24 CFR Part 58 (Environmental Review); NEPA

The City did not complete required environmental reviews under 24 CFR Part 58 before committing CDBG funds to projects. Federal funds cannot be obligated until the environmental review process is complete — this is a sequencing violation, not a substantive environmental issue.

---

### Finding 5 (2024-008): ESG — Obligation/Expenditure/Payment Requirements
**Severity: MODERATE**
**Regulation:** 24 CFR 576.203 (ESG Obligation Requirements)

The City did not comply with ESG obligation, expenditure, and payment timing requirements.

---

### Finding 6 (2024-009): HOME — Housing Quality Standards / Noncompliance
**Severity: MODERATE**
**Regulation:** 24 CFR 92.251 (Property Standards)

HOME-funded units did not meet housing quality standards at initial occupancy or during ongoing monitoring.

## PART 2: STANDARDS OF CREATION — Were Compliance Documents Created Correctly?

### Finding 7 (NEW): CDBG Payroll — Missing Time-and-Effort Documentation Standard
**Standard:** 2 CFR 200.430(i) (Standards for Documentation of Personnel Expenses); OMB Uniform Guidance time distribution requirements
**What the standard requires:** Employees who work on multiple cost objectives (e.g., split between CDBG and general fund) must maintain personnel activity reports or equivalent documentation that: (a) reflects an after-the-fact distribution of actual activity, (b) accounts for total activity for which the employee is compensated, (c) is prepared at least monthly, and (d) is signed by the employee and a supervisory official.
**What likely happened:** The payroll expenditure control failure (Finding 1) almost certainly means the City lacks compliant time-and-effort documentation. Without it, the City cannot demonstrate that the methodology for charging payroll to CDBG meets the creation standard defined by the Uniform Guidance. This is not just missing controls — it is documentation that was never created to standard.
**Impact:** HUD can disallow any payroll charges to CDBG that are not supported by compliant time-and-effort documentation. This converts from an audit finding to a potential repayment obligation.

---

### Finding 8 (NEW): CDBG Procurement — Missing Written Procurement Standards
**Standard:** 2 CFR 200.318(a) (General Procurement Standards); 2 CFR 200.319 (Competition); 2 CFR 200.320 (Methods of Procurement)
**What the standard requires:** Non-federal entities must document written procurement procedures that conform to applicable state, local, and tribal laws and regulations, and the standards of the Uniform Guidance. These procedures must address: conflict of interest, full and open competition, cost/price analysis for purchases above the simplified acquisition threshold, and SAM.gov verification.
**What likely happened:** The SAM verification failure (Finding 2) indicates the City's written procurement procedures either don't include SAM verification as a required step, or the procedures exist but aren't being followed. Either way, the procurement procedures themselves don't meet the creation standard. If they exist but aren't followed, that's a Finding 2 issue. If they don't require SAM verification, the procedures themselves were not created to the standard required by 2 CFR 200.318(a).
**Impact:** This elevates the procurement finding from "they forgot to check SAM" to "their procurement system was never designed to catch this." A corrective action plan that only adds SAM checking misses the deeper issue.

---

### Finding 9 (NEW): CDBG Reporting — Federal Reports Not Prepared Per HUD IDIS Standards
**Standard:** HUD IDIS (Integrated Disbursement and Information System) data entry standards; CDBG Annual Performance Report (CAPER) preparation requirements; 24 CFR 91.520 (Performance Reports)
**What the standard requires:** CDBG grantees must prepare the Consolidated Annual Performance and Evaluation Report (CAPER) using data from IDIS. The data entered into IDIS must reconcile to the general ledger. The CAPER must be prepared following HUD's CAPER template with specific required content fields. The report must undergo public comment before submission to HUD.
**What likely happened:** If internal controls cannot detect errors in federal reports (Finding 3), the reports themselves were likely not prepared according to HUD's standard preparation methodology, which includes a reconciliation step between IDIS data and accounting records. The creation standard for this document type requires a specific workflow: GL data → IDIS entry → reconciliation → draft CAPER → public comment → final submission. A failure at the internal control stage means the reconciliation step — which is part of the creation standard — was skipped or inadequate.
**Impact:** The CAPER and other federal reports may contain data that doesn't reconcile to Stockton's own accounting records. If HUD CPD pulls IDIS data and compares it to the City's financial statements during monitoring, discrepancies become a separate finding.

---

### Finding 10 (NEW): Environmental Reviews — Missing Environmental Review Record (ERR)
**Standard:** 24 CFR 58.38 (Environmental Review Record); HUD Environmental Review Procedures (CPD Notice 16-07)
**What the standard requires:** The Responsible Entity (the City) must prepare and maintain a written Environmental Review Record (ERR) for each project. The ERR must contain: project description, environmental assessment or exemption/categorical exclusion determination, public notice documentation, Request for Release of Funds (RROF), and HUD approval (Authority to Use Grant Funds — AUGF). The ERR must be completed BEFORE funds are committed.
**What likely happened:** The environmental review failure (Finding 4) indicates ERRs were either not prepared or not completed before fund commitment. Under the creation standard, the ERR is a specific document with specific required components created in a specific sequence. If the sequence was violated (funds committed before ERR completion), the document doesn't meet its creation standard even if it's later completed — because the standard requires the review to precede the commitment.
**Impact:** Projects where funds were committed before environmental clearance may need to be unwound or may require a HUD-approved remediation process. Late-completed ERRs do not cure the sequencing violation.

---

### Finding 11 (NEW): HOME — HQS Inspections Not Conducted Per HUD Inspection Protocol
**Standard:** 24 CFR 92.251 (Property Standards); HUD Housing Quality Standards Inspection Form (HUD-52580); Uniform Physical Condition Standards (UPCS)
**What the standard requires:** HOME-assisted units must meet HQS at initial occupancy. Inspections must be conducted using the HUD-prescribed inspection protocol, with specific room-by-room assessment, a standardized scoring methodology, and documented pass/fail determinations. The inspector must be qualified under the grantee's standards.
**What likely happened:** The HQS noncompliance (Finding 6) indicates either inspections weren't conducted, weren't conducted by qualified inspectors, or weren't documented using the standard protocol. Under the creation standard for HQS inspections, the inspection form itself must follow HUD's format, the inspector must be identified and qualified, and the results must be retained per record retention requirements.
**Impact:** Without properly documented HQS inspections, the City cannot demonstrate that HOME-funded units are habitable. This creates potential liability to tenants and potential repayment to HUD for units that don't meet property standards.

## PART 3: SOC CONTROLS — Can Stockton Verify Its Own Documentation?

### Finding 12 (NEW): Single Audit Independence and Quality — Auditor Rotation
**Standard:** Government Auditing Standards (Yellow Book, GAO-21-368G); 2 CFR 200.501 (Audit Requirements); AICPA Professional Standards AU-C Section 220
**What the SOC controls require:** The Single Audit must be conducted by an independent auditor who meets the independence requirements of Government Auditing Standards. The audit firm should be subject to peer review. The auditor must have sufficient understanding of federal programs to test compliance.
**What to verify:** Has Stockton's auditor been subject to peer review? How long has the current firm served as Stockton's auditor? Is there an auditor rotation policy? Given that 6 findings were identified across all three HUD programs in a single year, has the City evaluated whether prior-year audits should have identified emerging control weaknesses before they became findings?
**Impact:** If the current audit identified 6 findings, the question is whether prior-year audits missed early warning signs. This is not an accusation — it's a governance question the City should be asking.

---

### Finding 13 (NEW): CDBG Subrecipient Monitoring Documentation
**Standard:** 2 CFR 200.332 (Requirements for Pass-Through Entities); 24 CFR 570.501(b) (Subrecipient requirements)
**What the SOC controls require:** If any CDBG funds pass through to subrecipients, the City must: evaluate subrecipient risk, monitor subrecipient activities, ensure subrecipient Single Audits are obtained and reviewed, and maintain documentation of all monitoring activities.
**Why this matters:** Four findings in CDBG alone raises the question of whether the internal control weaknesses extend to subrecipient monitoring. If the City can't control its own payroll and procurement, it's unlikely it can effectively monitor subrecipients. If any CDBG funds flow through subrecipients, the absence of documented monitoring is a SOC integrity gap.
**Impact:** Subrecipient monitoring deficiencies are a separate finding category that HUD CPD flags during monitoring visits. The current findings suggest the City should self-assess its subrecipient monitoring before HUD does.

---

### Finding 14 (NEW): Cross-Program Internal Control Integrity Assessment
**Standard:** 2 CFR 200.303 (Internal Controls); COSO Internal Control — Integrated Framework (2013); GAO Standards for Internal Control in the Federal Government (Green Book)
**What the SOC controls require:** Internal controls must be designed, implemented, and operating effectively across all federal programs. The control environment — tone at the top, risk assessment, control activities, information and communication, and monitoring — must function as an integrated system.
**What the findings reveal:** Six findings across three HUD programs in one audit year is not six isolated issues. Under COSO/Green Book framework, it indicates a failure at the control environment level — the organizational commitment to internal control that enables all other control activities. Payroll controls, procurement controls, reporting controls, and environmental review controls all failed simultaneously. This pattern suggests the City either: (a) lacks a centralized compliance function for federal programs, (b) has compliance responsibilities fragmented across departments without coordination, or (c) experienced turnover or resource constraints that degraded control effectiveness across the board.
**Impact:** A corrective action plan that fixes each finding individually will close the audit findings but may not address the systemic control environment issue. If the root cause is structural, the findings will recur under different compliance areas in future audits. HUD CPD will recognize this pattern.

## What the Triple Constraint Found

The original 6 findings said: "The City violated these regulations."

The 8 new findings say:
- The documents the City is supposed to produce to demonstrate compliance were never created to the standard that defines how they must be made (Findings 7-11)
- The City's ability to verify and control its own compliance documentation has systemic gaps (Findings 12-14)
- The 6 original findings are symptoms of a deeper control environment failure that a finding-by-finding corrective action plan won't fix (Finding 14)

---

## What This Means For Stockton

**The corrective action plan we already prepared addresses the 6 original findings with specific remediation steps.** The 8 new findings from the triple constraint suggest the CAP should also include:

1. **Time-and-effort documentation system** — Not just better payroll controls, but a compliant system per 2 CFR 200.430(i) that produces documentation meeting the creation standard
2. **Procurement procedure rewrite** — Not just adding SAM checking, but rewriting written procurement procedures to meet 2 CFR 200.318(a) end-to-end
3. **Federal reporting workflow** — A documented GL-to-IDIS reconciliation process that meets HUD's CAPER preparation methodology
4. **Environmental review sequencing protocol** — A hard gate that prevents fund commitment before ERR completion, with documented ERRs per 24 CFR 58.38
5. **HQS inspection standardization** — Qualified inspectors using HUD-52580 protocol with documented results
6. **Subrecipient monitoring assessment** — Self-assessment of monitoring controls if any CDBG funds pass through subrecipients
7. **Control environment assessment** — A one-time COSO/Green Book assessment of the City's federal program control environment to identify the structural root cause

**These additions would move the engagement from "close the findings" to "fix the system that created them."** That's the difference between a corrective action plan and a compliance transformation.
