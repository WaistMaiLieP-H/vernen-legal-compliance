Mr. Garcia,

I wanted to follow up on the analysis I provided regarding the City's FY2024 Single Audit findings. After our exchange, we completed a system update on our end that brought our audit framework to full capacity. When we re-ran the City's findings through the updated system, it identified 8 additional issues that the original analysis missed.

The original analysis looked at regulatory compliance — whether the City met the requirements. The updated system also evaluates whether compliance documents were created according to the federal standards that define how they must be made, and whether the City can verify the integrity of its own documentation. That's where the 8 new findings came from.

Here is what the updated analysis found:

**Document Creation Standards (5 new findings):**

1. The payroll finding (2024-004) likely means time-and-effort documentation per 2 CFR 200.430(i) is missing or incomplete. Without it, HUD can disallow payroll charges — converting an audit finding into a repayment obligation.

2. The SAM finding (2024-005) indicates the City's written procurement procedures may not include suspension/debarment checking as a required step under 2 CFR 200.318(a).

3. The reporting finding (2024-006) suggests the CAPER preparation process may not include a reconciliation step between the general ledger and IDIS data per HUD's methodology.

4. The environmental finding (2024-007) means Environmental Review Records were either not prepared or not completed in the required sequence under 24 CFR 58.38. Completing them after funds are committed doesn't cure the sequencing issue.

5. The HOME finding (2024-009) raises questions about whether HQS inspections followed HUD's prescribed protocol and documentation standards.

**System Integrity (3 new findings):**

6. If any CDBG funds pass through subrecipients, the control weaknesses identified in the original findings may extend to subrecipient monitoring under 2 CFR 200.332.

7. Six findings across three HUD programs in one audit year points to a control environment issue under the COSO/Green Book framework — the organizational structure that supports all other controls.

8. The pattern suggests the City should evaluate whether prior-year audits identified emerging weaknesses before they reached this level.

I should have caught these in the first analysis. Our system wasn't where it needed to be at that point, and I want to be straightforward about that. It is now, and the updated analysis with all 14 findings is attached.

We've prepared corrective action options at three levels, from closing the original 6 findings to a full compliance assessment with monitoring through the next audit cycle. We'd be glad to walk through any of it if the City is still considering outside support.
